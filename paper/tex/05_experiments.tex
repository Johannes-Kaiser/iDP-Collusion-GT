\section{Experimental Evaluation}
\label{sec:experiments}

We evaluate the PEG numerically across six experiments designed to expose the
structural properties identified in \cref{sec:analysis}.

\subsection{Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\paragraph{Mechanism solver.} For each action profile $\boldsymbol{\eps}$,
we compute the mechanism solution $(\sigma, p_1,\dots,p_n)$ by bisecting on
$\sigma$ so that the (C2) batch-size constraint is met, with each $p_g$ chosen
to make (C1) bind via Mironov-RDP composition~\cite{mironov2017renyi}. We then
compute each $A_i$ from the privacy-loss-distribution of the $I$-fold
$(p_i, \sigma)$-subsampled Gaussian using
\cite{google-dp-accounting}, which gives a numerically tight MIA advantage via
\cref{eq:adv}.

\paragraph{Scenarios.} Unless stated otherwise, all experiments use $n$
symmetric groups of $|G_g| = 5000$ samples each, expected batch size $E_b =
128$, $5$ epochs of training, and $\delta = 10^{-12}$. The action grid is
$\mathcal{A} = \{1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 64\}$. These choices broadly mirror the
empirical iDP scenarios of~\cite{kaiser2026your, boenisch2023have}.

\paragraph{Equilibrium reporting.} Where best-response dynamics converges,
we report the resulting pure Nash equilibrium. Where it cycles (as in the
aware regime), we report the time-average over the cycle of the last few
iterations; the same averaging is used in the comparative-statics figures.

\subsection{E1: payoff and advantage landscape, aware vs.\ naive}

\Cref{fig:E1} reports the $2$-player payoff and advantage grids in both
regimes. Two phenomena are visible. First, the aware-regime utility
(\Cref{fig:E1}, top) exhibits a saddle pattern that destroys the existence of a
pure NE in the symmetric setting: regardless of the choice of own $\eps_i$, the
own utility can always be improved by some unilateral move. Second, the
realised MIA advantage (\Cref{fig:E1}, bottom row) is materially different
across the two regimes: in the aware regime, players' own choices steer them
away from the extremes, whereas in the naive regime, the realised vulnerability
explodes at low $\eps_i$ because both players race to the smallest budget
without internalising its externality on themselves and others.

\begin{figure*}[!t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/fig_E1_payoff_grids.pdf}
\caption{E1: $2$-player payoff $u_1$ and realised MIA advantage $A_1$ as a
function of own ($\eps_1$, vertical axis) and other ($\eps_2$, horizontal
axis) budgets, in the aware and naive regimes. Naive players treat their
nominal $\eps_i$ as the privacy cost; aware players treat the realised
advantage $A_i$ as the privacy cost. The aware-regime utility (top-left) has
no clear best-response argmax along either axis, foreshadowing the
non-existence of a pure Nash equilibrium.}
\label{fig:E1}
\end{figure*}

\subsection{E2: 2-player asymmetric group sizes}
\label{sec:E2}

\Cref{fig:E2} reports the equilibrium budget and the realised MIA advantage
as a function of the dataset size ratio $|G_1|/|G_2|$. Because best-response
dynamics cycles in the aware regime, we report the time-average over the
last 5 iterations of best-response dynamics as the equilibrium proxy. Two
findings emerge. First, the minority group (lower $|G_g|$) consistently
suffers a higher realised MIA advantage, because its sampling rate $p_g$ is
pushed upward by the (C2) batch-size constraint when its own population is
small. Second, the equilibrium choice of $\eps$ depends on the relative
position in the dataset: a minority player has a strong incentive to declare
\emph{more} privacy (lower $\eps$), yet this drives the larger group's
sampling rate even higher, increasing the larger group's externality cost.

\begin{figure*}[!t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/fig_E2_asymmetric.pdf}
\caption{E2: equilibrium budget (left) and realised MIA advantage (right) as a
function of dataset size ratio $|G_1|/|G_2|$, in the aware regime.}
\label{fig:E2}
\end{figure*}

\subsection{E3: equilibrium versus number of players}
\label{sec:E3}

We extend to $n \in \{2,3,4,5\}$ symmetric aware players. \Cref{fig:E3}
reports the mean equilibrium budget and the mean realised MIA advantage at
the equilibrium. The mean realised advantage rises with $n$: more players
means more independent strategic interactions through the shared $\sigma$,
and the equilibrium drifts toward lower $\eps$ for everyone, raising
$\sigma$ but also raising each $p_g$ (by (C2)).

\begin{figure*}[!t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/fig_E3_nplayer.pdf}
\caption{E3: equilibrium budget (left) and mean realised MIA advantage (right)
as a function of the number of players $n$ in the symmetric aware-regime
setting.}
\label{fig:E3}
\end{figure*}

\subsection{E4: coalition Stackelberg attack}
\label{sec:E4}

We fix $n = 5$, designate player $0$ as the target with $\eps_t = 32$, and let
the remaining $n-1 = 4$ players form a coalition of size $k$, with the
$n-1-k$ non-coalition players fixed at $\eps = 32$. \Cref{fig:E4} reports
$A_t$ as a function of $k$. Consistent with
Proposition~\ref{prop:coalition-attack}, $A_t$ is monotone-increasing in $k$:
as the coalition grows, more of the dataset's total budget can be driven
downward, raising the target's sampling rate and its MIA advantage.

\begin{figure}[!t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/fig_E4_coalition.pdf}
\caption{E4: realised MIA advantage of the target as a function of coalition
size $k$. The target plays $\eps_t = 32$; the coalition's optimal $\eps_C$
is the smallest admissible budget at every $k$, recovering the budget-
manipulation attack of Kaiser et al.~\cite{kaiser2026your}.}
\label{fig:E4}
\end{figure}

\subsection{E5: naive vs.\ aware comparison}
\label{sec:E5}

\Cref{fig:E5} reports the equilibrium budget and the equilibrium MIA advantage
across $n \in \{2,3,4,5\}$ in both regimes. The naive regime delivers a higher
realised MIA advantage at every $n$: naive players ignore the externality,
and their best response is to declare a low $\eps$ which makes everyone less
private. Aware players partially internalise this externality and so the
equilibrium MIA advantage is lower (though not zero, since the externality is
still mediated by every individual player's own model-utility incentive).

\begin{figure*}[!t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/fig_E5_awareness.pdf}
\caption{E5: equilibrium budget and realised MIA advantage for $n \in
\{2,3,4,5\}$ in the naive and aware regimes. The naive regime always
yields a higher realised MIA advantage at the equilibrium.}
\label{fig:E5}
\end{figure*}

\subsection{E6: privacy externality surface}
\label{sec:E6}

Finally, we report the realised MIA advantage of a fixed target player as
the other group's budget and population proportion are swept.
\Cref{fig:E6} mirrors the analytical computation of
Figure~3 of \cite{kaiser2026your} but framed through the PEG. The target's
$A_t$ rises sharply as the other group's $\eps$ falls (strategically equivalent
to ``the colluders pick low budgets'') and as their relative proportion grows
(strategically equivalent to ``more colluders''). This is the
externality lemma made visible: any other player's privacy demand inflates the
target's vulnerability monotonically.

\begin{figure*}[!t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/fig_E6_surface.pdf}
\caption{E6: target's realised MIA advantage as a function of the other
group's budget $\eps_O$ and proportion. The target keeps $\eps_T \in \{4,
16\}$ fixed. The curves rise as $\eps_O$ falls (more privacy demanded by
others) and as the proportion of the other group rises.}
\label{fig:E6}
\end{figure*}
