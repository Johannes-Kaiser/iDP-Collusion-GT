\section{Equilibrium Analysis}
\label{sec:analysis}

\subsection{Monotonicity of the externality}

The privacy externality of sampling-based iDP is monotone in every other
player's budget under the binding mechanism constraints.

\begin{lemma}[Privacy externality]
\label{lem:externality}
Consider the sampling-based iDP mechanism of \cref{def:sampling-idp} on a
profile $\boldsymbol{\eps} \in \mathcal{F}$ at which constraints (C1) bind
for every group and (C2) binds at $E_b$. Then for every $i \neq j$,
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial A_i(\boldsymbol{\eps})}{\partial \eps_j} \;\leq\; 0.
\label{eq:externality-lemma}
\end{equation}
That is, lowering the budget of any other group $j$ (a more privacy-conscious
peer) weakly raises group $i$'s realised MIA advantage.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
We work with the R\'enyi-DP form of (C1), in which $\rho_g = 2 I \alpha\,
p_g^2 / \sigma^2$ at a chosen order $\alpha$. Since $\rho_g$ is monotone-
increasing in $\eps_g$, $p_g$ is determined by $\sigma$ and $\eps_g$ via $p_g
= (\sigma/\sqrt{2I\alpha})\,\sqrt{\rho_g(\eps_g)}$. Substituting into (C2),
\begin{equation}
\sum_g \frac{|G_g|}{M}\,\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{2I\alpha}}\,
\sqrt{\rho_g(\eps_g)} \;=\; \frac{E_b}{M},
\end{equation}
which gives $\sigma$ as a continuously differentiable, monotone-decreasing
function of any single $\rho_g$ (and so of any single $\eps_g$). Implicit
differentiation yields, for every $i \neq j$,
\(
\partial \sigma / \partial \eps_j \;<\; 0
\)
and
\(
\partial p_i / \partial \eps_j \;>\; 0,
\)
because if $\eps_j$ falls then $\sigma$ rises and so does $p_i$ via the (C1)
relation at fixed $\eps_i$.

The MIA advantage $A_i = \Adv(\mathcal{M}_{p_i,\sigma,I})$ is, for the
$I$-fold subsampled Gaussian, increasing in $p_i$ and decreasing in $\sigma$
along the privacy-profile envelope (this is exactly the statement that the
trade-off function of the subsampled Gaussian is sharper for larger sampling
rate or smaller noise multiplier~\cite{balle2020privacy, dong2022gaussian}).
The compound effect of $\partial \eps_j$ on $A_i$ via $(\sigma, p_i)$ along
the binding constraint surface is therefore non-positive, which is
\eqref{eq:externality-lemma}.
\end{proof}

\Cref{lem:externality} is the formal expression of the empirical finding of
Kaiser et al.~\cite{kaiser2026your}: choosing a low budget for oneself
mechanically inflates the realised privacy risk of every other contributor.
This monotonicity makes the strategic situation we analyse one of negative
externalities: every additional unit of privacy demanded by one player imposes
a non-negative cost on every other player.

\subsection{Nash equilibria in the naive regime}

A naive player's utility~\eqref{eq:utility-naive} depends on
$\boldsymbol{\eps}_{-i}$ only through $\sigma(\boldsymbol{\eps})$. Each player
therefore trades off the linear cost $\eps_i / \eps_i^{\max}$ against the
indirect effect of $\eps_i$ on the shared $\sigma$. In the symmetric setting
we observe three pure Nash equilibria.

\begin{observation}[Naive equilibria]
\label{obs:naive-ne}
For the symmetric $2$-player aware PEG with $B_i = C_i = 1$, $\eps^{\max}=64$,
the action grid $\mathcal{A} = \{1,2,4,8,16,32,64\}$, $|G_1|=|G_2|=5000$,
$E_b=128$, $I=390$, $\delta=10^{-12}$ and $V(\sigma) = 1/(1+\sigma)$, the
naive-regime PEG admits three pure Nash equilibria:
\[
\boldsymbol{\eps}^*_{\text{naive}} \in \{(1, 8),\,(4, 4),\,(8, 1)\}.
\]
The symmetric equilibrium $(4,4)$ has realised $A_1 = A_2 = 0.123$. The
asymmetric equilibria have one player at $A = 0$ and the other at $A = 0.275$
\textnormal{(see Table~\ref{tab:NE})}.
\end{observation}

The presence of asymmetric equilibria is the formal expression of the
budget-manipulation attack: the configuration $(1, 8)$ is exactly the
``one player sacrifices privacy for the other'' regime. The asymmetry is
self-stabilising---neither player has a profitable unilateral deviation,
because the high-$\eps$ player gains both model utility \emph{and} no
externality cost (the nominal-cost-only utility~\eqref{eq:utility-naive} does
not see the realised $A_2$).

\subsection{Equilibria in the aware regime}

The aware regime internalises the realised advantage through
$A_i(\boldsymbol{\eps})$. The resulting game has a qualitatively different
structure: in the same symmetric setting as Observation~\ref{obs:naive-ne}, all
pure Nash equilibria lie at low budgets and are asymmetric by one grid step.

\begin{observation}[Aware equilibria]
\label{obs:aware-ne}
Under the same parameters as Observation~\ref{obs:naive-ne}, the aware-regime
PEG admits two pure Nash equilibria:
\[
\boldsymbol{\eps}^*_{\text{aware}} \in \{(1, 2),\,(2, 1)\}.
\]
Both equilibria are asymmetric by exactly one grid step, yield $A_1 + A_2
\approx 0.131$, and dominate the naive symmetric equilibrium $(4, 4)$ in
total realised MIA: aware players reach an equilibrium with roughly half the
realised population-level vulnerability of naive players
\textnormal{(see Table~\ref{tab:NE})}.
\end{observation}

The picture is the following. Both aware players have the incentive to pick a
small $\eps$ for their own realised privacy, but they cannot both pick the
smallest grid value $\eps = 1$: at the symmetric point $(1, 1)$, $\sigma$
explodes (very small $\eps$ everywhere) and the model-utility term $V(\sigma)$
collapses. Each player's best response is therefore to deviate \emph{upward}
by one grid step, recovering model utility while shifting the externality onto
the other player. The result is an anti-coordination equilibrium: one player
plays $\eps = 1$, the other plays $\eps = 2$, and the realised advantages
$(A_1, A_2)$ are unequal but small in aggregate. Crucially, both equilibria
are at the low end of the action grid---aware players move \emph{toward}
privacy at the equilibrium, not away from it.

\begin{remark}
Observations~\ref{obs:naive-ne} and~\ref{obs:aware-ne} together imply that the
attack on iDP of Kaiser et al.~\cite{kaiser2026your} is precisely the
asymmetric naive equilibrium $(1, 8)$ or $(8, 1)$. The asymmetric equilibrium
is rational, not adversarial: any naive player who consults their iDP contract
and sees only $(\eps_i, \delta)$ will gladly accept a low $\eps_i$ and is
indifferent to the realised $A_j$ on their peer. The aware regime is the only
way to internalise the externality, and even then the equilibrium remains
asymmetric---i.e., the aware regime mitigates but does not eliminate the
externality.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Coalition Stackelberg and the attack recovery}

\begin{proposition}[Coalition best response recovers the attack]
\label{prop:coalition-attack}
Fix a target $t$ and a coalition $C$ of size $k = |C|$, with $\lambda \to
\infty$ in Definition~\ref{def:coalition} (the coalition cares only about
maximising the target's vulnerability). The coalition's best response is
$\eps_i = \eps^{\min}$ for every $i \in C$, and the target's MIA advantage
$A_t$ is monotone-increasing in $k$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
By Lemma~\ref{lem:externality}, $\partial A_t / \partial \eps_i \leq 0$ for
every $i \in C$. Setting every $\eps_i$ as low as feasible maximises $A_t$
pointwise on $\mathcal{A}^{k}$. The monotonicity in $k$ is the same
lemma applied to adding a member to $C$: doing so strictly enlarges the set of
budgets that pull $\sigma$ upward, raising $A_t$.
\end{proof}

Proposition~\ref{prop:coalition-attack} establishes that the budget-
manipulation attack of Kaiser et al.~\cite{kaiser2026your} is precisely the
\emph{rational} best response of any coordinated subset of data holders,
regardless of intent. Whether the coalition is malicious is irrelevant: any
group of aware players whose joint utility weights their target's harm equally
to their own utility will pick the same action vector.
