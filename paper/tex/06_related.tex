\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Individual differential privacy.}
The classical $(\eps,\delta)$-DP guarantee~\cite{dwork2006calibrating} applies
uniformly to every record. Personalised DP, introduced by Jorgensen et
al.~\cite{jorgensen2015conservative}, allows per-record budgets and was first
realised by attribute deletion and weighting. The sampling-based realisation
of Boenisch et al.~\cite{boenisch2023have} (NeurIPS 2023) is the most widely
used today and is the realisation under which the privacy externality we study
arises. Filtering-based iDP using R\'enyi filters~\cite{feldman2021individual}
also supports per-record budgets but suffers from catastrophic forgetting and
is therefore rarely used in practice. Our game framework is specific to the
sampling-based realisation; we discuss the extension to sensitivity-based iDP
in \cref{sec:discussion}.

\paragraph{Excess privacy risk and mechanism comparison.}
Mechanisms calibrated to the same $(\eps,\delta)$ can have very different
privacy profiles and thus very different realised
risks~\cite{kaissis2024beyond, balle2020privacy, dong2022gaussian}.
The $\Delta$-divergence introduced by Kaissis et al.~\cite{kaissis2024beyond}
provides a notion of approximate Blackwell-dominance that quantifies the
worst-case excess MIA advantage across mechanism choices and is used by
Kaiser et al.~\cite{kaiser2026your} to motivate an $(\eps_i, \delta, \Delta)$-iDP
contract that bounds excess vulnerability. Our PEG is complementary: rather
than restricting mechanism design, we explain why
the realised excess vulnerability emerges as the equilibrium of self-interested
play under the current iDP contract.

\paragraph{Collusion vulnerabilities in iDP.}
Kaiser et al.~\cite{kaiser2026your} empirically demonstrate that lowering some
contributors' budgets in sampling-based iDP raises the MIA advantage on every
other contributor's data, even though every $(\eps_g, \delta)$-DP guarantee
continues to hold. They model two attacks: a centralised budget-manipulation
attack and a distributed collusion attack. Our work places both attacks
inside a single game-theoretic model and shows they correspond to two
versions of the same Stackelberg best response, executed either by a single
designated leader (the model trainer) or by a coordinating coalition.

\paragraph{Game theory of privacy and collaborative learning.}
Pej\'o et al.~\cite{pejo2019together} introduced a two-player game on
collaborative learning, with players choosing privacy parameters $p \in [0, 1]$
in an arbitrary privacy mechanism. Their notion of \emph{Price of Privacy} is
the relative accuracy loss at the equilibrium versus no privacy. Crucially,
their privacy mechanism's effect on accuracy depends on \emph{both} players'
choices, but its effect on \emph{privacy} depends only on the player's own
choice---there is no privacy externality. Our PEG transplants their
methodology into a setting where the externality is in the privacy direction,
not the accuracy direction. We retain their conceptual contribution (the Price
of Privacy) and extend it to the Price of Excess Vulnerability
(Definition~\ref{def:poev}).

Earlier work studied related game-theoretic privacy questions in different
settings: Ioannidis and Loiseau~\cite{ioannidis2013linear} model linear
regression with private features as a non-cooperative game; Chessa, Grossklags
and Loiseau~\cite{chessa2015game} study non-monetary incentives in data
analytics; Pawlick and Zhu~\cite{pawlick2016stackelberg} model the
machine-learner-vs-data-perturber conflict as a Stackelberg game. None of
these treat the privacy externality of a shared sampling mechanism, which is
specific to the sampling-based realisation of iDP.

\paragraph{Membership inference attacks.}
The MIA advantage as we use it follows the formalisation of
Carlini et al.~\cite{carlini2022membership}, who introduced the likelihood-
ratio attack (LiRA) as a tight empirical estimator. The privacy onion
effect~\cite{carlini2022privacy} explains why not every data point is equally
vulnerable, even at fixed privacy budget; our framework predicts the
\emph{average} realised MIA advantage and abstracts over per-record onion
heterogeneity.

\paragraph{Federated learning and incentive design.}
A separate literature studies privacy-aware incentive design in federated
learning: a server commits to incentives, and heterogeneous clients respond
with their own privacy choices, often modelled as a Stackelberg game. This
includes incentive-driven heterogeneous FL with per-client DP and
client-sampling models. Our framework can be viewed as a foundational
description of the strategic floor that any such incentive design must rest
on: it tells the mechanism designer the equilibrium that emerges in the
absence of incentives, which is what any incentive scheme needs to improve
upon.
